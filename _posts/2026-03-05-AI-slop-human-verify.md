AI is getting weirdly good at the easy parts of knowledge work, while stubbornly bad at the part that actually matters: knowing whether something is true. That mismatch is the center of my current view on how to use these tools without letting them quietly lower your standards.

Even in academia, avoiding AI is often a poor strategic decision. Like it or not, the hidden rule of the profession is “publish or perish.” If job security depends on output, then tools that plausibly raise productivity should at least be tested. I don’t like what those incentives do to science, but pretending they don’t exist doesn’t make them go away.

The productivity gains from AI range from tedious tasks (skimming papers, generating plots, proofreading, paraphrasing) to genuinely challenging ones (writing code, brainstorming experiment designs). You don’t need AI for everything, but it’s hard to deny how useful it can be. I used to lose half a day tweaking a plot because of a library conflict. Now I can often get to a workable solution in about 30 minutes with AI (it still struggles; some libraries are just painful). The hours saved can go toward work that actually benefits from human attention.

That said, I think of current AI systems as powerful “slop machines.” They’re great at producing outputs, but they can’t reliably verify whether those outputs are correct. Even when you ask for verification, they may generate something that looks like a check while quietly smuggling in errors. To non-experts it can feel plausible; to experts it often reads like confident nonsense. But that doesn’t mean the output is useless, it means the workflow has to treat AI as a fast generator, not an authority.

This is especially clear in the recent excitement about AI doing mathematical research. A good example is First Proof (arXiv:2602.05192), which tests models on research-level questions with answers temporarily encrypted. People trying this kind of evaluation often report the same pattern: most outputs are wrong, but some are suggestive enough to be useful with expert guidance. As of March 2026, the emerging view is that AI helps most on problems humans could solve, but don’t have time to grind through. 

So the human role shifts. Instead of spending most of our time on production, we spend more time on verification, judgment, and refinement; turning messy output into something reliable. This lines up with the distinction emphasized in Some Simple Economics of AGI (arXiv:2602.20946): when execution becomes cheap, the binding constraint becomes verification bandwidth; the time and attention required to audit and steer outputs at scale. 

In that world, I don’t think scientists are simply “replaced.” If anything, expertise becomes more valuable, because someone has to separate signal from noise and decide what’s actually true. The adaptation is real, though: we should lean into the skills that age well; verification, judgment, taste, and refinement, while offloading the grind when it makes sense.
